# Summary: Auditory Tasks in Cognitive Research (Pages 1-20)

**Source**: Baldwin, C. L. (2012). Chapter 6: Auditory Tasks in Cognitive Research. In *Auditory Cognition and Human Performance*. CRC Press.

---

## Executive Summary

This chapter provides a comprehensive review of how auditory tasks have been employed across cognitive research domains for over a century. The author emphasizes a **critical gap** in most auditory cognition research: **researchers frequently neglect to report acoustic characteristics (presentation level/loudness) and participant sensory acuity**, which can profoundly affect results and interpretation, particularly in aging research.

---

## 1. Historical Context and Foundation (Pages 93-97)

### Key Historical Contributions

**Early Language Studies**:
- **Psamtik I (7th century BC)**: Described as conducting the "first psychological study" - attempted to identify original human language through auditory deprivation experiment with feral children (Hunt, 1993)

**Phrenology and Localization**:
- **Franz Josef Gall (1758-1828)**: Identified 4 separate auditory-verbal brain areas:
  1. Memory of words
  2. Sense of language/speech
  3. Sense of sounds/music
  4. Poetic talent

**Aphasia Research**:
- **Paul Broca (1825-1880)**: Documented "Tan" (Leborgne) - inability to **produce** speech despite intact comprehension (Finger, 1994)

- **Carl Wernicke (1848-1905)**:
  - Documented opposite pattern: inability to **comprehend** speech despite fluent production
  - **Advanced theory**: Language and thought are separate processes (Gage & Hickok, 2005)
  - **Modern relevance**: "Memories are represented in the same neural pathways initially used to encode the stimulus information" - strikingly similar to theories by Squire (1986) and Damasio (1989)

## 2. Dichotic Listening Tasks (Pages 97-98)

**Historical Importance**: Key technique in 1950s-1960s selective attention research during the "cognitive revolution" (Broadbent, 1958; Cherry, 1953; Moray, 1959; Treisman, 1960)

### Modern Applications:
- Measure of cerebral dominance (Rahman et al., 2008)
- Index of attentional control (Andersson et al., 2008)
- Neuropsychological tests for attention deficits (Diamond, 2005)

### Key Findings (Moray, 1969):
1. **Similarity effect**: More similar messages = harder to separate
2. **Physical difference aids separation**:
   - Different loudness levels
   - Different pitch
   - Different spatial locations (including dichotic = different ears)
   - Different speaker gender (Egan et al., 1954; Treisman, 1964b)

**Critical finding**: Dichotic presentation increases signal-to-noise ratio by **as much as 30 dB** compared to monaural presentation (Egan et al., 1954; Moray, 1969)

**Practical implication**: In male-dominated environments (e.g., flight cockpits), verbal warnings in **female voice** are more salient

## 3. Encoding and Retrieval Processes (Pages 98-99)

**Key Study**: Craik et al. (1996) - Effects of divided attention on aurally presented words

### Findings:
- **Divided attention during ENCODING** → significantly disrupted memory
- **Divided attention during RETRIEVAL** → increased reaction time to concurrent task
- **Encoding is more vulnerable** to dual-task interference than retrieval (Anderson et al., 1998; Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998)
- **Age effects**: Older listeners showed particularly dramatic dual-task costs during encoding

**Voice consistency effect** (Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998):
- Same voice during encoding AND retrieval → significantly better recall
- Supports **multiple memory trace models** of speech comprehension (Ju & Luce, 2006; Luce et al., 2000)

## 4. Working Memory: Complex Span Tasks (Pages 99-100)

### Listening Span Task

**Foundational Study**: Daneman & Carpenter (1980)
- **Problem addressed**: Traditional digit span/word span showed little relationship with reading/listening comprehension
- **Solution**: Task requiring both **processing** AND **storage**
  - Process series of sentences + make veracity judgment
  - Store final word from each sentence
- **Result**: Much more predictive of language comprehension than simple span tasks (meta-analysis: Daneman & Merikle, 1996)

### Modern Applications:
1. **Educational**: Predicts reading ability and learning disabilities in children (Swanson & Howell, 2001; Henry, 2001)

2. **Nutrition effects** (Morria & Sarll, 2001):
   - A-level students skipping breakfast vs. glucose drink
   - **20 minutes post-glucose**: Significantly better listening span performance
   - **Implication**: Importance of breakfast for cognitive performance (Benton & Jarvis, 2007; Pollitt, 1995)
   - Effect extends to college students (Hewlett et al., 2009)

3. **⚠️ CRITICAL FINDING** (Baldwin & Ash, 2010):
   - **Direct relationship** between presentation intensity (loudness) and assessed listening span capacity
   - **Particularly evident in older adults (60-80 years)** vs. young (18-31 years)
   - **Implication**: Presentation level (PL) MUST be reported and controlled

## 5. N-Back Tasks (Pages 101-102)

**Design**: Monitor incoming stream of stimuli, respond to items **n** positions back
- Difficulty manipulated by varying **n** (1-back = easiest, 3-back = harder)
- Can be verbal or nonverbal
- Can monitor identity OR location

### Auditory-Spatial N-Back (University of Helsinki)
**Method**: Interaural intensity differences (~17 dB) create perception of left/right/center location

**Sleep Study** (Steenari et al., 2003):
- **Poorer sleep quality** (actigraph-measured activity) → poorer working memory at all load levels
- **Shorter sleep duration** → poorer performance on 2-back (most difficult)

**Developmental Study** (Vuontela et al., 2003):
- Working memory improved across ages 6-13 years
- Females matured slightly faster (to age 10)
- **Auditory-spatial working memory matures more slowly than visual counterpart**

**⚠️ Critical Gap**: "Despite the reliance on speeded sensory-perceptual processing in these working memory tasks, all too frequently little if any care is taken to ensure that participants have been equated for sensory acuity"

## 6. Psychological Refractory Period (PRP) (Pages 102-104)

**Paradigm**: Speeded responses to two tasks in rapid succession (Pashler, 1998a; Welford, 1952)

**Finding**: Response to second task is delayed when it follows first task closely in time

### Driving Applications (Levy & Pashler, 2008; Levy et al., 2006):

**Study 1**: Brake response + concurrent auditory/visual choice task
- **Stimulus Onset Asynchrony (SOA)** significantly affected brake response times
- **Implication**: Performing auditory OR visual task just prior to brake event → slower brake response

**Study 2**: "Change-task" paradigm (low-priority auditory tone + high-priority brake response)
- **60% of participants** failed to withhold tone response >80% of the time despite explicit priority instructions
- **Mistakenly performing auditory task first** → significantly slowed brake response
- **Real-world implication**: Even when aware of priority, drivers may be unable to stop completing lower-priority tasks (e.g., explaining why they're hanging up instead of just dropping the phone)

**Conclusion**: Evidence for **central processing bottleneck** in information processing

## 7. Mental Workload Assessment (Pages 104-107)

### Auditory Secondary Tasks

**Delayed Digit Recall** (Mackworth, 1959; Zeitlin, 1995):
- Random digit sequence (1-9) presented at intervals
- Participant recalls previously presented digit (e.g., 2-back)
- **Sensitive to**: Driving environment changes, traffic density, adverse weather (Zeitlin, 1995)
- **Implementation**: One digit every 2 seconds for 2 minutes, 98% accuracy criterion required

**Mental Arithmetic**:
- **Kahneman version**: Three digits → add 3 to each → report in order
- **Baldwin & Schieber/Harms version**: Two-digit number → subtract smaller from larger (e.g., 57 → 2)
- **Sensitivity**: Urban vs. rural driving (Harms, 1991), driving simulation difficulty (Baldwin & Schieber, 1995)
- **Advantage**: Auditory input + verbal output = minimal interference with visual/motor tasks

### Auditory ERP Indices: P300 Oddball Paradigm (Pages 106-107)

**Design**:
- Stream of tones: 80% standard (distractor), 20% target
- Target tones elicit larger P300 amplitude
- As primary task workload increases → P300 amplitude to targets decreases

**Applications**:
1. **Flight workload** (Kramer et al., 1987): Distinguished operator workload levels (wind turbulence, takeoff/landing vs. level flight)
2. **Hypermedia difficulty** (Schultheis & Jameson, 2004): P300 amplitude (but not pupil diameter) reduced for difficult text
3. **Driving in fog** (Baldwin & Coyne, 2005): P300 sensitive to visibility changes when performance/subjective measures were not

**Irrelevant Probe Task**:
- Ask participant to **ignore** periodic auditory probe
- N1 component sensitive to flight task difficulty (Kramer et al., 1987)
- **Advantage**: No overt response required

## 8. Neuropsychological Tests (Pages 107-109)

### PASAT (Paced Auditory Serial Addition Task)

**Design**: Series of single-digit numbers → sum each two consecutive → report aloud
- Example: 5, 7, 3, 9 → responses: 12, 10, 12

**Purpose**: Originally for traumatic brain injury assessment; now common test of information processing speed and working memory (Tombaugh, 2006)

**⚠️ Important Caveats**:
1. Affected by age, speech, and language abilities
2. **Modality effects**: Substantially better performance with VISUAL vs. auditory presentation (Wickens, 1984)
   - Suggests **resource competition** between auditory encoding and verbal response
   - May assess "rapid auditory attention switching" rather than pure processing speed

3. **Critical limitation**: "Age-related changes in cognitive performance void of careful assessment of sensory acuity must be regarded with extreme caution. Degradations in performance associated with age could be attributed to peripheral or central hearing mechanisms rather than cognitive deficits"

**Clinical Use**: Pharmaceutical effects on cognition (opioids: Sjogren et al., 2005; ADHD: White et al., 2005)

### RAVLT (Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test) (Page 109)

**Design**:
- Read aloud List A (15 words) → free recall → repeat 4 more times
- Present List B → recall
- Recall List A without re-presentation

**Basis for**: California Verbal Learning Test, WMS-III, Hopkins Verbal Learning Test

**Administration**: 10-15 minutes, easy to administer

### Halstead-Reitan Neuropsychological Test Battery (Pages 109)

**Use**: Assess neurological impairment (brain trauma, mental disorder, alcoholism)

**Two Auditory Tests**:
1. **Speech-Sounds Reception Test**: Identify prerecorded nonsense words (rhyme with "ee")
2. **Seashore Rhythm Test**: Discriminate rhythmic beat patterns
   - Originally from 1939 "Measures of Musical Talent"
   - Used for attentional processing assessment (Taylor-Cooke & Fastenau, 2004)
   - Clinical validity sometimes questioned (Sherer et al., 1991)

## 9. Neurophysiological Investigations (Pages 110-112)

### Prepulse Inhibition (PPI)

**Phenomenon**: Reduction in startle response to strong stimulus when preceded by weaker stimulus

**Clinical Significance** (Graham, 1975):
- Index of **central inhibitory mechanisms** / **sensorimotor gating**
- **Schizophrenia**: Reduced PPI (Grillon et al., 1992; Perry et al., 1999)
  - Exception: Female patients did not differ from controls (Kumari et al., 2004)
- **Sex differences**: Females exhibit lower PPI than males (rats and humans)

### Event-Related Potentials (ERPs)

**Mismatch Negativity (MMN)**:
- **Design**: Automatic brain response to any discriminable change in repetitive stimuli
- **Timing**: Peaks 100-180 ms after deviant stimulus
- **Characteristics**:
  - Amplitude increases with deviation magnitude
  - Occurs with both addition AND removal of acoustic elements
  - **Does not require focused attention or overt response** (Naatanen, 1992)

**Applications**:
1. **Nonverbal populations**: Infants, comatose patients (can assess without active participation)
2. **Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex lesions**: Reduced MMN, especially for ipsilateral stimuli (Alho et al., 1994)
3. **Sleep deprivation**: MMN amplitude decreases gradually after 24-36 hours wakefulness (Raz et al., 2001)
4. **Distractibility in children**: Younger children (8-9 years) more distracted than older (10-13 years) by irrelevant tones (Gumenyuk et al., 2004)

## 10. Auditory Tasks in Cognitive Aging Research (Pages 112-113)

### ⚠️ CRITICAL METHODOLOGICAL CONCERNS

**The Problem**:
- Aging accompanied by decreased auditory acuity (Corso, 1963; Fozard & Gordon-Salant, 2001)
- Acoustic presentation level (PL) impacts mental workload (Baldwin & Struckman-Johnson, 2002)
- **Neglecting sensory acuity and stimulus PL can seriously undermine interpretation**

**Example Study** (Federmeier et al., 2003):
- **Finding**: Older adults took 25 ms longer in early sensory stages, then "made up time" in lexical access (400 ms), but spent extra 200 ms on contextual processing
- **Problem**: Authors did NOT report participant sensory acuity or sentence presentation levels
- **Result**: Impossible to determine if early delay was due to:
  - Central factors (auditory association cortex)
  - OR peripheral factors (outer/middle/inner ear sensitivity thresholds)

### Theoretical Frameworks:

**Sensory-Cognitive Interaction Theory (SCIT)** (Baldwin, 2002):
- Additional mental effort to process degraded sensory stimuli depletes resources from later processing stages

**Effortfulness Hypothesis** (McCoy et al., 2005; Wingfield et al., 2005):
- Sensory impairments exacerbate or can be mistaken for cognitive impairments

**Clinical Evidence** (Maastricht Aging Study - van Boxtel et al., 2000):
- Controlling for age, education, processing speed
- **Hearing acuity was a strong predictor** of auditory verbal learning test performance

## Important Methodological Recommendations

### For Auditory Task Research:

1. **ALWAYS report**:
   - Presentation level (PL) in dB SPL
   - Participant sensory acuity (pure-tone thresholds)
   - Background noise characteristics

2. **Control for**:
   - Age-related hearing loss (especially >60 years)
   - Individual differences in sensory capabilities
   - Signal-to-noise ratio

3. **Consider**:
   - Modality effects (auditory vs. visual presentation)
   - Resource competition (auditory encoding + verbal output)
   - Physical stimulus characteristics (pitch, gender, spatial location)

4. **Avoid**:
   - Attributing performance differences solely to cognitive factors without ruling out sensory contributions
   - Using auditory tasks without baseline sensory assessment
   - Comparing groups with different sensory capabilities without equating stimulus salience

## Concluding Observations

**Author's Main Argument**:
"All too frequently, researchers have neglected to consider the mental workload requirements of processing acoustic information. Factors such as individual differences in sensory capabilities challenge interpretation of many of these investigations."

**Critical Quote** (Page 112-113):
"Age-related changes in cognitive performance void of careful assessment of sensory acuity must be regarded with extreme caution. Degradations in performance associated with age could be attributed to peripheral or central hearing mechanisms rather than cognitive deficits."

This chapter serves as a **methodological critique** as much as a literature review, emphasizing that auditory cognition research must account for the **mental workload of sensory processing itself**, not just higher-order cognitive demands.
